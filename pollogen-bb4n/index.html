01 — The brief

A different audience than the social work. A different motion.

Pollogen's organic social program speaks to distributors, aestheticians, and consumers: the people who pull the brand through the market. This proposal is about a separate, higher-stakes conversation entirely.

The goal here is to reach senior executives inside tier-1 accounts, the L'Oréals and Philips of the world, and put a single idea in front of them: Pollogen's technology can sit inside their products, as a white-label or OEM partner. That is not a feed. It is a boardroom. And you do not reach a boardroom by posting more often.

You reach it with account-based marketing. A discipline built to identify a small, named set of high-value accounts, map the people inside them who matter, and surround those people with content built specifically for them. This document explains how we would run that program for Pollogen over six months, what it costs, and what it produces month by month.

02 — What ABM is

Most marketing casts a wide net. ABM picks the fish first.

Account-based marketing flips the usual logic of demand generation. Instead of putting content in front of a broad audience and waiting to see who raises a hand, ABM starts with a short list of specific companies you have decided are worth winning, then works backward to the people inside them.

  • /01

    It starts with named accounts, not audiences

    The first move is a defined target account list: a finite set of companies, chosen on fit and reachability, not a demographic. For Pollogen that means the specific tier-1 brands whose product lines could carry Pollogen technology. We treat each one as a market of its own.

  • /02

    Inside each account is a buying committee, not a buyer

    A white-label decision at a company like L'Oréal is never made by one person. It moves through a committee: someone who owns innovation, someone who owns the technology, someone who owns the commercial case, sometimes procurement and regulatory too. ABM identifies these roles (the "buyer personas") and speaks to each one differently, because each one cares about something different.

  • /03

    The message is matched to the person

    A Chief Innovation Officer wants to see the science. A CTO wants to understand integration and IP. A CFO wants the unit economics. In ABM the creative, the landing page, and the email a person sees are built for their function, not for a generic "audience." That personalization is the entire point. It is what makes a cold account feel like a warm one.

  • /04

    Marketing and sales run the play together

    ABM is the only marketing discipline designed to hand off cleanly to a salesperson. The whole program exists to make the sales conversation easier: either by generating a direct lead, or by warming the account so thoroughly that when the Pollogen rep makes contact, the brand is already recognized. We will define that handoff explicitly so nothing falls between the two teams.

The function we target is the one open question. Pollogen helps us answer it.

You asked us to leave the exact role to be defined: Chief Innovation Officer, CTO, CFO, Head of Product, or a combination. That is the right call, and it is exactly what a persona-definition workshop in Month 1 is for. We come with the structure and the research. Pollogen confirms which functions actually sign off on an OEM partnership. Everything downstream, every asset and every targeting setting, follows from that decision.

03 — The strategy

Three pillars. One direction of travel.

The program runs on three pillars in sequence. Define the account and the people in it. Personalize to their function. Then either generate the lead or warm the ground for sales. Each pillar feeds the next.

PILLAR 01

Define the account, define the committee

We build the target account list with Pollogen, tier it by fit and reachability, and run the persona workshop that resolves which functions we are actually selling to. Nothing gets made until we know who it is for.

PILLAR 02

Personalize to the function

Every ad, landing page, email, and hero asset is matched to the persona. Scientific framing for innovation and R&D. Technical framing for the CTO. Financial framing for the CFO. The account sees a Pollogen that already understands its world.

PILLAR 03

Generate the lead, or warm the ground

Two ways to win, measured separately. Either the program produces direct top-tier leads, or it builds enough brand familiarity inside the account that the Pollogen salesperson's outreach lands warm. We commit to both and report on both.

03b — Two ways to win

Quantity or warmth. We measure each on its own terms.

You framed success two ways, and they are genuinely different goals with different metrics. Rather than blur them, we run a dual-KPI model so the six-month review is never an argument about which one counted.

TRACK A — VOLUME

Generate top-tier leads

For when the priority is getting names and contact into the pipeline at scale across the account list. Direct response, form fills, asset downloads, meeting requests.

What we report
  • Leads / MQLs generated by account
  • Cost per lead vs. benchmark
  • Asset and calculator completions
  • Meeting requests booked
TRACK B — WARMTH

Warm the account for sales

For when the priority is air cover: building enough familiarity inside the account that the Pollogen rep's outreach is a warm conversation, not a cold one. This is the higher-value game for a long OEM sales cycle.

What we report
  • Account penetration (people reached per account)
  • Engagement by named account and persona
  • Repeat exposure / frequency per contact
  • Warm-reply rate on sales outreach into engaged accounts

At kickoff we agree which track leads. Most tier-1 OEM programs weight toward Track B, because the deal size justifies patience and the sales cycle is long. We can rebalance at the Month 3 checkpoint once real data exists.

04 — The asset system

Same product. A different argument for every chair in the room.

Personalization is not a logo swap. Each persona gets creative, a landing page, and an email sequence written to the thing that person is actually accountable for. The Pollogen science stays constant. The argument changes.

05 — The stack

The targeting layer that puts the work in front of the right names.

ABM is only as good as its ability to reach specific people at specific companies. The platform layer is how a named account list becomes real impressions on real decision-makers.

LinkedIn, company and title targeting

The core channel for reaching named accounts by company and by job function. Where committee members at tier-1 brands actually are in a professional mindset.

Account-based display / person-level ads

A platform such as Influ2 to serve ads to named individuals, the air-cover layer that builds familiarity across the whole committee, not just whoever clicks.

Intent signals

Where available, third-party intent data to prioritize accounts already showing interest in OEM, device manufacturing, or beauty-tech, so spend concentrates where it is warmest.

CRM and lead routing

Clean handoff into Pollogen's CRM so a generated lead or an engaged account reaches the right salesperson with context attached. We confirm the CRM at kickoff and build routing to match.

We have run exactly this stack before, LinkedIn and person-level display layered against CRM intent signals, on a named-account program for an enterprise client. The mechanics are proven. What we tailor here is the account list, the personas, and the science-led creative.

06 — The 6-month plan

Six months, phase by phase.

The arc is simple: build the foundation, launch, then optimize and warm. The three payment milestones (advance, launch, completion) map directly onto the three phases below.

Phase 1 · Foundation50% due in advance

  • Kickoff and ABM working session: confirm objectives, weight Track A vs. Track B, agree definitions of a lead and a "warm" account.

  • Target account list build. With Pollogen, assemble and tier the named accounts (Tier A / B / C) by fit and reachability. L'Oréal, Philips, and peers anchor the list.
  • Account research dossiers for Tier-A accounts: org structure, committee names where findable, recent public initiatives and signals.
  • Messaging architecture per persona and the asset production plan.
  • Platform and CRM setup: LinkedIn, account-based display, intent and routing wiring confirmed.
Month 2Build
  • Produce the per-persona campaign assets: ad creatives and docuads, personalized landing pages, email sequences.
  • Begin the hero assets: scope, design, and start the build of the white-label ROI / margin calculator and the clinical / technical value model.
  • Stand up tracking, audiences, and account lists inside each platform. Build the lead-routing path into Pollogen's CRM.
  • Internal QA and Pollogen review of all creative and claims before anything goes live (science and IP claims confirmed with Pollogen SMEs).
Phase 2 · Launch25% due at launch
Month 3Go live
  • Campaigns launch across LinkedIn and account-based display against the Tier-A list.
  • First hero asset (the ROI / margin calculator) goes live and becomes the centerpiece of the commercial-persona track.
  • Email sequences begin; landing pages live per persona.
  • Baseline data collection: reach, engagement by account, early leads. No targets asserted yet, pure measurement.
  • Month 3 checkpoint: first real read on which accounts and personas are responding. Rebalance Track A vs. Track B if the data argues for it.
Phase 3 · Optimize & warm25% due on completion
Month 4Optimize
  • Optimize against baseline: shift budget and creative weight toward the accounts and personas showing engagement.
  • Second hero asset (clinical / technical value model) goes live, opening the innovation and product-persona track fully.
  • Creative refresh round one: retire what is not landing, double down on what is.
  • Expand reach within engaged Tier-A accounts to more committee members (deepening penetration for Track B).
Month 5Warm & hand off
  • Concentrate spend on the warmest accounts. This is where air cover converts into sales readiness.
  • Sales handoff playbook delivered to the Pollogen team: which accounts are warm, who has engaged, what they saw, and how to open the conversation.
  • First warm sales outreach into engaged accounts, coordinated with Pollogen, so we can measure warm-reply rate.
  • Optional third hero asset deployed if scoped.
Month 6Review & recommend
  • Full six-month performance report against both tracks: leads and CPL on Track A, account penetration and warm-reply rate on Track B.
  • Account-by-account read: which tier-1 targets moved, which need a longer runway.
  • Asset library handover: all hero tools, landing pages, and creative documented and transferred for ongoing sales use.
  • Recommendation for Month 7 onward: which accounts to keep pursuing, where to expand the list, whether to scale media.
Always on, every month

Campaign management and optimization, weekly internal monitoring, a monthly account-level report and review with Pollogen, and a standing point of contact. The rigor is identical in every month; what changes is whether the focus is building, launching, or warming.

09 — The takeaway

ABM does not fail because the accounts are too big. It fails when the message is too generic.

Pollogen has the one thing that makes a white-label pitch to L'Oréal or Philips credible: real, defensible science. The work is to put that science in front of the exact people who decide, in the exact language each of them speaks, often enough that the Pollogen name is familiar before the first sales call. That is what this program does.
