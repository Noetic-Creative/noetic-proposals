01 / The Opportunity

Three reasons this earns the investment.

Today, output quality is gated by a small group of senior people. Every great deliverable still requires Tamir, Caro, Jose, Gonzalo, Alex, or Nicole personally. That ceiling will not scale into 2026.

  • /01

    Distributes the quality ceiling

    Skills move tacit expertise into reusable infrastructure. The only real path to handling larger accounts without cloning our senior people, or letting quality drift as we grow.

  • /02

    Defensible competitive moat

    A library of 40+ Noetic-specific skills, account-aware and curated, is something a competitor cannot copy in a quarter. It is also a strong recruiting story for the AI-fluent specialists we want to attract.

  • /03

    Compounds with our AI-first posture

    The skills we build this year become the substrate for the workflows we automate next year. We have to write the playbook before the machine can run it.

02 / The Four-Layer Framework

How the library is structured.

Four layers of abstraction. Each one sits on top of the one below it. The meta-skill enables the foundational frameworks. The frameworks enable the plays. The plays serve specific accounts. Order matters.

L1 / Meta

How skills get written

The shared Claude Project that interviews each author, structures their thinking, enforces a consistent template, and produces a draft skill ready for review.

1 skill · Owned by Jose + Jackie
L2 / Foundational

Strategic mental models

The cross-cutting frameworks that everything else gets built on. Strategic Brief, ICP and Voice, Campaign Architecture, Lifecycle Design.

4 skills · Owned by department leads
L3 / Plays

The work-doing layer

Discrete repeatable jobs that produce outputs. Diagnostics and audits, content generators, performance reports, production workflows. Where AI leverage lives.

27 skills · Highest reuse, broad ownership
L4 / Account-Specific

Per-client knowledge bases

One skill per top client, capturing ICP, voice, brand, restrictions, contacts, current strategic priority. Makes every team member instantly fluent on an account.

10 skills · AM, Caro, Alex jointly
Critical principle

If we get the meta-skill right, everything else compounds. If we get it wrong, we have a graveyard of mediocre skills nobody trusts. L1 is non-negotiable.

06 / Risks & how we manage them

The six places this can fail.

/ 01

First-draft quality varies wildly

Most people write bad skills the first time. Descriptions do not trigger reliably, procedures stay vague, examples are missing. The meta-skill is the only real defense. Expect 30 to 50% of first drafts to need substantial rework.

/ 02

Adoption is not automatic

Building a skill does not mean people use it. Track usage. If a skill has fewer than three uses in six weeks, it is dead weight. Either fix it or pull it from the library.

/ 03

Confidentiality drift

L4 account skills contain client IP, positioning, sometimes commercial terms. Default rule: freelancers do not get access to L4 unless explicitly assigned to that account. Bake this in from day one.

/ 04

Senior time investment is real

A solid skill takes 4 to 8 hours of the author's time, plus review time. Across Tier 1, roughly 60 to 80 hours of senior team capacity over 8 to 10 weeks. Budget it. Do not pretend it is free.

/ 05

Founder drift

Tamir will have ideas for skills he wants to write himself. The discipline is to stay in the sponsor and final-reviewer seat. If the founder starts authoring, the program quietly becomes another Tamir-built tool, and the team disengages. Most likely failure mode.

/ 06

Stale skills become liabilities

A skill referencing a client we no longer have, or a tool we deprecated, is worse than no skill at all. Each skill has a named owner. Owners review their skill quarterly. Build the cadence in from week one.
